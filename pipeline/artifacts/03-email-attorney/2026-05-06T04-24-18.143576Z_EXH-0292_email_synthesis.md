## Section 1: Evidence Summary

This March 20, 2020 email from Fifth Third Bank VP Leonard Mayersky forwards a COVID-19-era Paycor payroll relief offer to *both* JZ and LG at their Rowboat Creative email addresses. On its face, the communication is transparent and the subject matter is legitimate — a vendor offering three months of free payroll services to businesses affected by the pandemic. However, the email's evidentiary value lies not in its surface content but in three contextual signals: (1) Mayersky's reference to "the big stuff" that needed to be "finished" first, indicating prior, potentially undisclosed financial dealings between Mayersky and Rowboat; (2) the database subdirectory label "LG-and-Leonard-Mayersky-communicate-corporate-email-servers-no-JZ," suggesting this email was captured within a larger pattern of LG-Mayesky backchannel communications occurring without JZ's participation; and (3) the exhibit's database tag BANK_FRAUD_TRANSFERS, confirming its relevance to the financial misconduct allegations at the core of this case. The email tends to prove that Leonard Mayersky was an active financial intermediary for Rowboat Creative during the period when the alleged fraudulent transfers occurred, and that significant undisclosed banking matters ("the big stuff") were in progress between Mayersky and at least one member of the LLC.

## Section 2: Claim Alignment

### Fiduciary Breach — SUPPORTING
**Governing Authority:** 805 ILCS 180/15-3 (member-managed LLC fiduciary duties: loyalty, care, good faith and fair dealing).

**Element Analysis:**
- **Duty of Loyalty:** The subdirectory provenance — "LG-and-Leonard-Mayersky-communicate-corporate-email-servers-no-JZ" — places this email within a corpus of communications between LG and Mayersky that bypassed JZ. If LG used Mayersky as a banking partner to orchestrate transfers or conceal financial information from his co-member, this email is one link in that chain. Mayersky's reference to "the big stuff" that needed to be "finished" before discussing payroll relief strongly implies material financial matters were underway that had not been disclosed to both members equally.
- **Duty of Care / Good Faith:** The payroll relief offer was a legitimate cost-saving opportunity for the LLC during a national crisis. If LG intercepted, delayed, or failed to act on this offer while simultaneously using Mayersky's banking relationship for self-dealing, that would constitute a breach of the duty of care and good faith.
- **Key Deposition Question:** "Mr. Mayersky, what was 'the big stuff' you referenced needing to finish, and with whom at Rowboat Creative had you been discussing it?"

### Wire Fraud (18 U.S.C. § 1343) — SUPPORTING
**Governing Authority:** 18 U.S.C. § 1343: (1) scheme to defraud, (2) use of interstate wire communications, (3) specific intent to defraud.

**Element Analysis:**
- **Interstate Wire:** The email was transmitted via Outlook/Exchange servers (`CH2PR19MB3768.namprd19.prod.outlook.com`), satisfying the wire element.
- **Scheme to Defraud:** This email alone does not prove a scheme. However, it is circumstantial evidence that Mayersky was a financial confidant actively involved in Rowboat's banking affairs at the relevant time. If the "big stuff" involved transfers that form the basis of the wire fraud claim, this email corroborates the relationship and communication channel through which the scheme was executed. The BANK_FRAUD_TRANSFERS tag from the Mayersky Smoking Gun database independently confirms investigators viewed this communication as part of the fraud pattern.
- **Specific Intent:** Not established by this email alone; requires linkage to the broader transfer evidence.

### Fraud (Common Law) — BACKGROUND
**Governing Authority:** *Siegel v. Levy Org. Dev. Co.*, 153 Ill. 2d 534 (1992): (1) false statement of material fact, (2) known or believed false, (3) intent to induce reliance, (4) actual reliance, (5) damages.

This email contains no false statement. The Paycor offer was real, and Mayersky's forwarding of it is facially benign. The email's relevance to common-law fraud is limited to establishing the banking relationship context.

### Conversion — BACKGROUND
**Governing Authority:** *Cirrincione v. Johnson*, 184 Ill. 2d 109 (1998).

No direct evidence of unauthorized assumption of control over LLC property. The email's value is contextual — it places Mayersky in a position to facilitate or have knowledge of conversion if LG used banking channels to misappropriate funds.

### Breach of Contract — TANGENTIAL

### Accounting — BACKGROUND

### Unjust Enrichment — TANGENTIAL

### RICO (Civil) — SUPPORTING (Pattern Evidence)

If wire fraud and mail fraud predicate acts are established elsewhere, this email is relevant to demonstrating the *means and method* of the enterprise — specifically, the use of a Fifth Third Bank insider (Mayersky) to facilitate or obscure financial misconduct. The subdirectory label strongly suggests a systematic practice of LG communicating with Mayersky outside JZ's view, which is probative of an enterprise operating with secrecy and coordination.

### Mail Fraud (18 U.S.C. § 1341) — BACKGROUND
This email was transmitted electronically, not via postal mail. Relevance is limited to pattern-of-conduct evidence if mail was used for related financial communications.

### Computer Fraud (CFAA, 18 U.S.C. § 1030) — NOT APPLICABLE

## Section 4: Admissibility Analysis (FRE)

### FRE 401/402 — Relevance
**Admissible.** The email is relevant to multiple claims in the case. It tends to make the existence of a banking relationship between Mayersky and Rowboat Creative more probable, and it provides context for the alleged fraudulent transfers. The "big stuff" reference is probative of undisclosed financial activity. Under the low bar of FRE 401 ("any tendency" to make a fact of consequence more or less probable), this email clears the threshold easily.

### FRE 403 — Prejudice vs. Probative Value
**Should survive a 403 challenge.** The probative value is moderate — the email is not a "smoking gun" on its own but is a meaningful piece of a larger pattern. Any prejudice to LG is limited because the email on its face appears innocuous; its inculpatory value derives from context and the subdirectory label, not from inflammatory content. The "LG-and-Leonard-Mayersky-communicate" label may draw a 403 objection if offered for its truth, but this goes to authentication and hearsay, not prejudice.

### FRE 801/802 — Hearsay

**Primary Pathway — FRE 801(d)(2)(A) (Party-Opponent Admission):**
- Mayersky's statements ("after we had the big stuff finished") are not LG's statements. However, the email was sent *to* LG and JZ. LG's responsive communications (if any) would be admissible as party-opponent admissions. The email itself, as a communication *to* a party-opponent, is not hearsay when offered to show notice, knowledge, or context rather than for the truth of the matter asserted.

**FRE 801(d)(2)(D) (Agent Statement):**
- If Mayersky was acting as an agent of Rowboat Creative (as its banker) in forwarding the Paycor offer and referencing "the big stuff," his statements may be admissible as statements by the LLC's agent on matters within the scope of the agency relationship. However, this is a closer call — Mayersky was a Fifth Third Bank employee, not a Rowboat employee, and agency must be established.

**FRE 801(d)(2)(E) (Co-Conspirator Statement):**
- If the broader evidence establishes a conspiracy between LG and Mayersky, Mayersky's statements in furtherance of that conspiracy would be admissible against LG. The subdirectory label supports this theory but is not independently sufficient.

**FRE 803(3) (State of Mind):**
- Mayersky's statement of intent to discuss Paycor "after we had the big stuff finished" reflects his then-existing state of mind and plan. Admissible under 803(3) to show that "big stuff" was pending — not for the truth of what that "big stuff" was.

**FRE 803(6) (Business Records):**
- The email is a business record of Rowboat Creative, captured from its corporate email servers. If properly authenticated by a records custodian, it is admissible under the business records exception. The "corporate-email-servers" reference in the subdirectory label supports this foundation.

### FRE 901 — Authentication
**Authenticable.** The email bears a Microsoft Exchange Message-ID header (`CH2PR19MB376837B7C6ED1E3FFFEE223BE1F50@CH2PR19MB3768.namprd19.prod.outlook.com`), date stamp, and sender/recipient fields. Authentication can be accomplished through:
- Testimony of a Rowboat Creative email administrator or records custodian (FRE 901(b)(1))
- Testimony of Leonard Mayersky confirming he sent the email (FRE 901(b)(1))
- Distinctive characteristics: the email address, signature block, and Paycor forward are consistent with Mayersky's role at Fifth Third Bank (FRE 901(b)(4))
- Metadata from the Mayersky Smoking Gun database extraction (FRE 901(b)(9) — system/process evidence)

### FRE 1002 — Best Evidence Rule
The native email file from the database satisfies the best evidence rule. A printout or PDF would be a duplicate admissible under FRE 1003 unless genuine questions are raised about authenticity.

### FRE 602 — Personal Knowledge
Mayersky had personal knowledge of his own communications and his relationship with Rowboat Creative. The Paycor email from Kim Hatton is also within Mayersky's personal knowledge as its recipient.
